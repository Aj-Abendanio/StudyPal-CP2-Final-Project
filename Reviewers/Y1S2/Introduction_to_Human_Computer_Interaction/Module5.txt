================================================================================
MODULE 5: DESIGNING FOR ERRORS
================================================================================

1. HUMAN CAPABILITIES AND LIMITATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- THE HUMAN ELEMENT: As humans, we see, hear, touch, and taste—but always with 
  physical and sensory limitations.
- COGNITIVE PROCESSES: We remember, learn, and solve problems, but we also 
  forget and make mistakes due to cognitive load or interference.
- HCI GOAL: Design systems that account for these limitations to minimize the 
  frequency and impact of human error.

2. MISTAKES VS. SLIPS (COGNITIVE ERRORS)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Human errors are categorized based on the intention behind the action:

A. MISTAKES (KNOWLEDGE-BASED)
- DEFINITION: The person has the wrong goal or a faulty mental model.
- CAUSE: Incorrect reasoning or lack of information.
- EXAMPLE: Thinking a file is saved automatically and closing the program 
  without clicking "Save."

B. SLIPS (ACTION-BASED)
- DEFINITION: The person has the right goal, but the execution of the action 
  is incorrect.
- CAUSE: Lack of attention, distraction, or poor motor control.
- EXAMPLE: Intending to click "Save" but accidentally clicking "Delete."



3. TYPES OF SLIPS (NON-COGNITIVE ERRORS)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- CAPTURE ERRORS: A frequently performed action "captures" a similar but 
  intended action (e.g., driving to work on a Saturday instead of the store).
- DESCRIPTION ERRORS: Performing the right action on the wrong object because 
  the two objects look similar (e.g., putting the lid of a juice bottle on 
  a milk carton).
- DATA-DRIVEN ERRORS: Automatic sensory data interferes with an action (e.g., 
  typing a number you hear while trying to type a different number).
- LOSS-OF-ACTIVATION ERRORS: Forgetting what you intended to do in the middle 
  of the action (e.g., walking into a room and forgetting why).
- MODE ERRORS: Occur when a device has different states where the same action 
  has different effects (e.g., typing while "Caps Lock" is accidentally on).

4. DESIGNING FOR ERROR PREVENTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- VISIBILITY: Make the state of the system clear so users don't make mode 
  errors.
- FORCING FUNCTIONS: Physical or logical constraints that make it impossible 
  to perform an error (e.g., a car that won't start unless it's in "Park").
- CONFIRMATION DIALOGS: Asking "Are you sure?" before a destructive action 
  (though users often click through these reflexively).
- UNDO/REDO: Providing a safety net that allows users to recover easily from 
  slips without losing work.



5. ERROR MESSAGES AND RECOVERY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- AVOID BLAME: Don't use "User Error" or "Invalid Input." Use polite, helpful 
  language.
- BE SPECIFIC: Tell the user exactly what went wrong and how to fix it.
- MINIMIZE WORK: If an error occurs in a long form, don't clear the entire 
  form; only highlight the field that needs correction.
- CONTEXTUAL HELP: Provide tooltips or balloon notifications near the source 
  of the error.
