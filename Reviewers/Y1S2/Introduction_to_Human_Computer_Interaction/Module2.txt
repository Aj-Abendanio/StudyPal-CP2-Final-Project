================================================================================
MODULE 2: NORMAN'S DESIGN PRINCIPLES
================================================================================

1. THE PSYCHOLOGY OF EVERYDAY THINGS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- GOOD DESIGN: Practical, easy to use, and self-explanatory. It requires no 
  extra labels or complex manuals.
- BAD DESIGN: Confusing, prone to errors, and frustrating for the user. Often 
  referred to as "Norman Doors" (doors that you pull when they look like they 
  should be pushed).

2. SIX CORE DESIGN CONCEPTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Donald Norman identified six fundamental principles that determine how well a 
user can interact with a product:

A. VISIBILITY
- Can the user see the current state of the device and the available actions?
- When functions are "hidden," users struggle to find them.
- QUESTION: "Can I see it?"

B. FEEDBACK
- Providing information back to the user about what action has been done and 
  what has been accomplished.
- Examples: A sound when a button is pressed, a progress bar, or a light.
- QUESTION: "What is it doing now?"



C. CONSTRAINTS
- Restricting the kinds of user interaction that can take place at a given 
  moment to prevent errors.
- Types: Physical (size/shape), Logical (reasoning), and Cultural (symbols).
- QUESTION: "Why can't I do that?"

D. MAPPING
- The relationship between controls and their effects in the world.
- Natural mapping takes advantage of physical analogies or cultural standards 
  (e.g., moving a slider up to increase volume).
- QUESTION: "Where am I and where can I go?"



E. CONSISTENCY
- Designing interfaces to have similar operations and use similar elements for 
  achieving similar tasks.
- Helps users transfer knowledge from one application or device to another.
- QUESTION: "I think I have seen this before?"

F. AFFORDANCES
- The attributes of an object that allow people to know how to use it.
- A chair "affords" sitting; a glass "affords" transparency; a handle 
  "affords" pulling.
- QUESTION: "How do I use it?"

3. CONCEPTUAL MODELS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- MENTAL MODEL: The user's internal understanding of how a system works based 
  on their prior experience and the cues provided by the system.
- DESIGN MODEL: The designer's mental model of how the system should work.
- SYSTEM IMAGE: What the user actually sees (the physical product, manuals).
- GOAL: The Design Model and the User's Mental Model should match through 
  a clear System Image.
