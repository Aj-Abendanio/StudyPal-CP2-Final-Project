Module 13: Design Thinking
1. Introduction
Design Thinking is a non-linear, iterative process that teams use to understand users, challenge assumptions, redefine problems, and create innovative solutions to prototype and test. In Computer Science, this methodology is critical during the Introduction to Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) and the early stages of software development. It shifts the focus from "What can we build?" to "What should we build to solve a human need?"
2. Key Concepts

- Empathy: The foundation of design thinking. It involves observing and engaging with people to understand their experiences and motivations. 
- Ideation: The process of generating a broad range of ideas and potential solutions without immediate judgment or criticism. 
- Prototyping: Creating scaled-down, inexpensive versions of the product (or specific features) to investigate the solutions generated in the previous stage. 
- Iterative Process: A cycle of testing and refining. Results from testing are used to redefine original problems and inform new iterations. 
- User-Centered Design (UCD): A framework of processes in which usability goals, user characteristics, environment, and tasks of users are given extensive attention at each stage of the design process. 
- Persona: A fictional character created to represent a user type that might use a site, brand, or product in a similar way.
3. Examples

- Empathize (Observation): Before building a banking app, developers sit with elderly users to watch them struggle with small font sizes. This "empathy" leads to a design with high-contrast modes and voice commands. 
- Define (Problem Statement): Instead of saying "We need to build a food app," the team defines the problem as: "Busy office workers need a way to order healthy lunches in under 3 minutes because their breaks are short." 
- Low-Fidelity Prototyping: Using paper and markers to draw the interface of a mobile app. This allows the team to test the "flow" of the app before a single line of code is written, saving thousands of dollars. 
- Testing (Feedback): A developer gives a prototype to a user and watches them try to find the "Checkout" button. If the user can't find it, the team goes back to the Ideate or Prototype phase to move the button.
4. Summary

- Design Thinking is human-centric , focusing on the user's needs first. 
- It follows five core stages: Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, and Test . 
- Prototyping is meant to be "fail fast, fail cheap"—identifying flaws before expensive development begins.
