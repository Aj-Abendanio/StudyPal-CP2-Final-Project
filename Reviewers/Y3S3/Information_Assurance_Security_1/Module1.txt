Module 16: Information Assurance & Security
1. Introduction
Information Assurance & Security (IAS) is the practice of protecting information and information systems from unauthorized access, use, disclosure, disruption, modification, or destruction. In an era of constant data breaches and cyber warfare, security is no longer an "add-on" feature; it is a fundamental requirement of software design. This module focuses on the CIA Triad , the psychological and technical aspects of threats, and the methods used to safeguard digital assets.
2. Key Concepts

- The CIA Triad: The core model of security: 
  - Confidentiality: Ensuring data is accessible only to those authorized to have access. 
  - Integrity: Ensuring data is accurate and has not been tampered with. 
  - Availability: Ensuring systems and data are accessible when needed. 
- Authentication vs. Authorization: 
  - Authentication: Verifying who you are (e.g., passwords, biometrics). 
  - Authorization: Verifying what you are allowed to do (e.g., admin permissions). 
- Cryptography: The science of encoding messages so that only the intended recipient can read them. 
- Encryption: The process of converting "plaintext" into "ciphertext" using an algorithm and a key. 
- Social Engineering: Manipulating people into divesting confidential information (e.g., Phishing). 
- Malware: Malicious software designed to damage or gain unauthorized access to a computer (e.g., Viruses, Ransomware, Trojans).
3. Examples

- Integrity (Hashing): When you download a software installer, the website often provides a "checksum" (a hash). If you run a hash function on your file and it matches the website's hash, you know the file was not tampered with by a hacker. 
- Asymmetric Encryption (Public Key): This is how HTTPS works. A website gives you its Public Key to encrypt your data, but only the website's Private Key can decrypt it. 
- Two-Factor Authentication (2FA): Combining "something you know" (password) with "something you have" (a code sent to your phone) to ensure a higher level of Authentication . 
- Phishing: An email that looks like it’s from your bank asking you to "verify your account" by clicking a link. This is a Social Engineering attack, not a technical hack.
4. Summary

- Security is balanced through the CIA Triad : Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability. 
- Most security failures are caused by human error or social engineering rather than technical exploits. 
- Encryption is the primary tool for maintaining confidentiality over public networks like the internet.
